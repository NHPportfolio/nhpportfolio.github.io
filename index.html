Source Governance Engine

A newsroom's source knowledge lives in individual reporters' heads, notebooks, and inboxes. When a reporter leaves, the relationships and the background too often leave with them, and under-resourced newsrooms, especially, struggle to answer who it has already spoken to or to see when coverage keeps leaning on the same few voices. The source governance engine is a browser sidebar that runs while a reporter works. Highlight a passage, and it reads the text, proposes who the source is and how they were used, and files that to a record the whole newsroom can search. Matching runs on embeddings, so it surfaces related past coverage even when the wording is different, and a confidence score sits on every inference. Each entry is saved only after the reporter confirms it. A second phase opens that vetted record to readers as a question-answering agent grounded in what the newsroom reported. In active testing at The Haitian Times.

Impact Generator

A small newsroom's proof of impact is buried in the stories it already published, and in the real-world outcomes those stories caused. Funders pay for outcomes, and outcomes rarely make it into the published text, so turning the work into funder language is slow and gets dropped first when staff is short. The impact generator takes a newsroom's own coverage, plus an outcome if there is one, and drafts the funder-facing version: a donor note, a grant letter of inquiry, an impact summary. It works only from what the newsroom reported, and it asks for any missing detail. It never invents a number or an outcome, and it never sends.

Sloane

Coaching and productivity apps hand everyone the same machinery, streaks, time-blocks, escalating reminders, that ignores what a specific user actually needs, so the structure ends up working against the user. Sloane is a coach that runs over text message, tailored to a specific user's needs and working style. It reads your calendar and the notes you keep in a Google Doc it treats as memory, then sends a short, oriented prompt in the morning and a reflection at night, and it takes replies as voice notes, transcribing them with Whisper so you can answer by talking. It orients around your actual day. It does not block your calendar, assign time slots, or escalate when you miss one. Building it around one real person is what forced the context and prompt decisions that make the coaching tailored to specific user needs.
